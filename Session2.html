The Electric Revolution

The shift to electric vehicles (EVs) is the most visible disruption. Once a niche concept, EVs are becoming mainstream, driven by environmental concerns, government incentives, and, crucially, a dramatic fall in the cost of their most expensive component: the battery. This cost reduction has been the single biggest enabler of the EV market.

Executive Briefing

A summary of key themes regarding Tesla's strategy and the evolving mobility landscape, based on the case study by Donald Sull and Cate Reavis.

I. Tesla's Ambitious Strategy and Production Challenges

"Master Plan" - High-End Entry, Mass-Market Scaling: Tesla's strategy was to "enter at the high end of the market... and then drive down market as fast as possible." This was seen in the progression from the expensive Roadster to the mass-market Model 3.

"Production Hell" and Automation Overreach: The scaling of the Model 3 proved "excruciating," with Musk admitting, "excessive automation at Tesla was a mistake... Humans are underrated."

Vertical Integration as a Core Competency: Unlike rivals, Tesla made up to 80% of components in-house to control quality and innovation, a strategy not seen since the early days of Ford.

Direct Sales Model: Tesla's company-owned stores challenged the traditional dealership model, creating a new customer experience but facing legal resistance.

II. The Traditional Automobile Industry: Challenges and Norms

Mature Market and Low Profit Margins: The U.S. auto market was a ~$270 billion industry in 2016 with flat growth and low operating margins (3-7%) for legacy OEMs.

High Barriers to Entry and Exit: New entrants required immense capital (~$6B), while high employment and specialized assets made it difficult for incumbents to exit.

Dominance and Vulnerability: In 2018, GM, Ford, and Chrysler held nearly 46% of the market, while Tesla had just 0.2%. However, low customer loyalty and an unpopular dealership model created vulnerabilities.

III. The Changing Face of Mobility: Disruptive Trends

Electrification (EVs): Driven by falling battery costs (down 80% from 2010-2017) and government incentives, forcing all OEMs to invest heavily. Tesla's proprietary Supercharger network provided a key advantage over the limited public infrastructure.

Autonomous Driving: The race to develop self-driving cars shifted the industry's focus from mechanics to software, creating "computers on wheels" and attracting tech giants.

Connectivity: Connected car revenues were projected to surge, creating new business models based on data and in-car services.

Mobility-as-a-Service (MaaS): A fundamental shift from ownership to on-demand usage, with ride-hailing and sharing services threatening the traditional sales model, especially in urban areas.

Frequently Asked Questions

A detailed breakdown of the most common questions about Tesla's strategy, production, and the competitive landscape.

What was Tesla's initial strategy for entering the car market, and how did its vehicle offerings evolve?

Tesla's "Master Plan" aimed to enter the high-end market first to fund progressively cheaper models. It began with the $100,000 Roadster (2008), followed by the Model S (2013) and Model X (2015), before targeting the mass market with the Model 3 (baseline $35,000).

What challenges did Tesla face in its production process, particularly with the Model 3?

Tesla experienced "production hell" with the Model 3 due to over-reliance on automation, which Musk called a mistake. The company's high degree of vertical integration (making 80% of parts in-house) and skipping a "beta" production phase led to bottlenecks, quality issues, and manual re-work on the assembly line.

How does Tesla's sales and service model differ from traditional automakers?

Tesla sells directly to consumers through company-owned stores with salaried staff, bypassing the independent dealership model. This has led to legal battles in several states. Service is streamlined due to fewer mechanical parts, with many updates and fixes delivered via over-the-air software updates. Tesla also built its own proprietary Supercharger network.

What major trends are reshaping the traditional automobile industry?

The industry is being reshaped by four key trends: 1) **Electrification**, driven by falling battery costs; 2) **Autonomous Driving**, shifting focus to software; 3) **Connectivity**, creating new revenue from in-car services; and 4) **Mobility-as-a-Service**, a shift from ownership to on-demand usage via ride-hailing and sharing.

What is Tesla's "Master Plan, Part Deux," and how does it envision the future of mobility?

Unveiled in 2016, it envisions a future with fully autonomous (Level 5) vehicles that can operate in a shared fleet, earning money for their owners when not in use. This concept aligns with the "mobility-as-a-service" model, where consumers buy transportation by the mile or trip, potentially leading to a significant drop in private car ownership.
